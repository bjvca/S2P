# Technical Note: Soil-Test Comparability and Application Error in the S2P Impact Evaluation

April 2026

## 1. Purpose

This note summarizes the implications of using different soil-testing approaches across treatment arms in the Space to Place (S2P) impact evaluation. It builds on the pre-analysis plan (PAP), the existing technical note on inter-laboratory differences, and Jonathan Atkinson's April 2026 comments on soil-test variability and recommendation systems.

The central issue is whether application error can be used as a causal outcome when the benchmark for "correct" fertilizer application is derived from soil tests and recommendations generated by different testing systems across experimental arms.

## 2. Experimental Context

The S2P trial randomly assigned villages to three arms:

- Control: no soil test, no customized recommendation, and no voucher during treatment implementation.
- T1: soil test, customized fertilizer recommendation, and extension advice.
- T2: T1 package plus a fertilizer voucher tied to the recommended fertilizer blend.

The PAP identifies application error as the primary fertilizer-use outcome. Application error is defined conceptually as the difference between the quantity of fertilizer or nutrients actually applied by the farmer and the quantity recommended or implied by the soil test.

After implementation, soil tests were also collected for the control group to construct shadow recommendations and to compare soil fertility across experimental arms. However, the treatment and control soil samples were not analyzed using the same testing approach:

- T1 and T2 samples were analyzed by Cropnuts.
- Control samples were analyzed by FES.

In addition, control samples were collected later than treatment samples. As a result, testing approach and sample timing are mechanically associated with treatment status.

## 3. Identification Problem

The main econometric problem is that lab/testing approach is perfectly collinear with treatment status. Any treatment-control difference in soil-test-derived variables may reflect:

- the treatment itself;
- differences between Cropnuts and FES measurement methods;
- spectral versus wet-chemistry differences;
- differences in extraction methods or calibration;
- differences in the agronomic model translating soil status into fertilizer requirements;
- sampling-date differences;
- rainfall or amendment effects between sampling rounds;
- spatial heterogeneity in parent material or soil-forming processes;
- sampling or handling error.

This is not a standard omitted-variable problem that can be solved by controlling for lab identity. Since lab identity predicts treatment arm, a lab fixed effect would absorb the treatment comparison of interest.

Nor is it necessarily a simple calibration problem. Without a bridge sample in which the same soil samples are tested by both Cropnuts and FES, the mapping between testing systems is not identified from the experimental data.

## 4. Implications for Main Outcomes

The soil-test comparability issue does not threaten outcomes measured identically across all arms at endline. These include:

- fertilizer adoption;
- fertilizer quantities applied;
- nutrient quantities applied, when derived from farmer-reported product use and common nutrient-content conversions;
- product-level fertilizer use;
- timing and method of fertilizer application;
- yields;
- value of production;
- profits;
- cost-benefit measures.

These outcomes should remain the basis for the main causal interpretation of the RCT.

The issue is specific to outcomes whose benchmark is derived from soil tests or lab-generated recommendations, especially application error.

## 5. Two Application-Error Concepts

Application error can be operationalized in two different ways.

### 5.1 Behavior Versus Soil Characteristics

This version asks whether farmers apply more nutrients when their soils are more deficient. The benchmark is a soil-test variable such as nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, pH, or other nutrient indicators.

This version can be made less sensitive to lab-level differences by using within-lab percentile ranks, provided the monotonicity assumption is credible. Under monotonicity, each lab may report different levels, but both preserve the ordering of samples.

This produces an ordinal measure of alignment rather than a physical-units measure of over- or under-application.

### 5.2 Behavior Versus Nutrient Requirements

This version asks whether farmers apply the amount of nutrients required to meet the recommendation. The benchmark is a requirement in kg/ha or an equivalent product recommendation.

This is more policy-relevant because both sides of the comparison can be expressed in agronomic units. However, it introduces an additional source of bias: labs may use different models to translate soil measurements into nutrient requirements. The existing technical note shows that requirement differences can be larger than raw soil-measurement differences, especially for potassium and phosphorus.

Therefore, direct treatment-control comparisons using lab-provided nutrient requirements are not clean causal estimates unless the recommendation models are made comparable or the comparison is explicitly treated as assumption-dependent.

## 6. Interpretation of Jonathan Atkinson's Comments

Jonathan's comments reinforce the identification concern. His main substantive points are:

- The study was not designed to compare or calibrate soil-testing approaches.
- Cropnuts and FES differ not only in measurement precision but potentially in testing approach and agronomic interpretation.
- Soil chemistry may also vary over time and space for reasons unrelated to lab performance.
- Recommendation outputs may be less sensitive than raw soil-test values because recommendations are constrained by target yields, product availability, and practical dosage increments.
- Given these constraints, treatment-control application-error estimates should not be overinterpreted as clean econometric outcomes.

The important implication is not that application error must be dropped. Rather, it should be reported with the correct hierarchy of credibility.

## 7. Recommended Analysis Hierarchy

### 7.1 Primary Causal Outcomes

The main paper should prioritize outcomes measured consistently across all arms:

- fertilizer adoption;
- quantities of fertilizer applied;
- nutrient quantities applied using common conversion factors;
- use of recommended or relevant fertilizer products;
- timing and method of application;
- yield;
- value of production;
- profits;
- cost-benefit.

These outcomes preserve the experimental interpretation of treatment-control comparisons.

### 7.2 Cleanest Application-Error Comparison

The cleanest application-error comparison is T1 versus T2. Both groups received soil tests and recommendations from the same testing/recommendation system. Therefore, application-error differences between T1 and T2 are not confounded by the Cropnuts/FES split.

This comparison answers whether adding the voucher improved alignment with the recommendation among farmers who received the soil-test and advice package.

### 7.3 Treatment-Control Application Error

Treatment-control application-error estimates should be treated as sensitivity or exploratory analyses. They can still be informative, especially because application error was listed in the PAP, but they should not carry the main causal claims.

At minimum, the following variants can be reported:

- raw application error using available lab/recommendation outputs;
- rank-adjusted application error using within-lab percentile ranks;
- linearly rescaled application error, if used, clearly labeled as relying on stronger assumptions;
- product-level adherence or relevance outcomes that are less dependent on exact nutrient calibration.

The preferred adjustment is the rank-based version because it requires monotonicity rather than cardinal comparability. Linear rescaling may be useful as a robustness check, but it assumes that observed differences can be represented as stable shift-and-scale differences between labs.

## 8. Application-Error Measures to Construct

For any application-error benchmark, the following measures should be considered:

- signed error: actual application minus recommended application;
- absolute error: absolute value of signed error;
- squared error: signed error squared;
- oversupply indicator: actual application exceeds recommendation by more than a defined tolerance;
- undersupply indicator: actual application falls short of recommendation by more than a defined tolerance;
- product-level adherence: farmer used the recommended fertilizer product or a close substitute.

Signed, absolute, and squared error answer different questions. Absolute and squared error summarize distance from the recommendation, while signed error and over/under indicators identify whether the deviation is due to over-application or under-application.

Tolerances should be specified before estimation, especially if recommendations are binned in practical units such as 50 kg increments.

## 9. Balance and Comparability Tests

Once the underlying data are available, balance or comparability tests should be conducted for nutrient deficiency and requirement variables. These tests should include at least:

- treatment-arm means for each soil-test and recommendation variable;
- standardized mean differences;
- treatment-control and T1-T2 differences;
- joint orthogonality tests where feasible;
- distributional plots by arm and lab/testing approach;
- missingness rates and merge success rates by arm.

These tests should be described as comparability diagnostics, not as conventional baseline balance tests. Because treatment status is collinear with lab/testing approach, observed differences cannot be uniquely interpreted as true soil imbalance.

The tests can still help classify variables into categories:

- variables that appear broadly comparable across testing systems;
- variables with moderate shifts that may be usable under rank-based assumptions;
- variables with large shifts, detection-limit issues, or likely method incompatibilities;
- variables that should be excluded or used only descriptively.

## 10. Recommended Paper Language

A concise way to describe the issue in the paper is:

> Application error was pre-specified as a primary fertilizer-use outcome. However, control-arm soil samples were collected later and analyzed using a different laboratory/testing approach than treatment-arm samples. As a result, treatment-control comparisons of soil-test-derived benchmarks may be confounded by testing-system differences. We therefore treat treatment-control estimates of application error as sensitivity analyses rather than primary causal evidence. Our main causal interpretation relies on outcomes measured consistently across arms. We emphasize application-error comparisons between T1 and T2, where the testing and recommendation system is common, and report treatment-control application-error estimates under alternative harmonization assumptions for transparency.

## 11. Bottom Line

The RCT remains valid for outcomes measured consistently across arms. The soil-test issue affects soil-test-derived outcomes, not the full evaluation.

Application error should remain visible because it is part of the PAP and is substantively important. However, treatment-control application-error estimates should not be presented as primary causal evidence unless heavily qualified. The defensible strategy is to:

- use directly measured fertilizer, production, and profit outcomes for main causal claims;
- emphasize T1 versus T2 for application-error analysis;
- report treatment-control application-error estimates as sensitivity analyses;
- implement rank-based harmonization as the preferred adjustment;
- treat linear rescaling as a stronger-assumption robustness check;
- add product-level adherence and over/under-application measures to make the mechanism analysis more interpretable.
